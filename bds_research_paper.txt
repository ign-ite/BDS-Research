\begin{abstract}
Apache Spark job scheduling in heterogeneous cloud environments
demands simultaneous optimisation of cost, energy efficiency,
throughput, deadline adherence, and resource utilisation.
Verma et al.\ (2025) proposed a distributional deep reinforcement
learning (DRL) framework using C51 and Rainbow DQN with fixed
Dirichlet reward weights. Three limitations remain open: reward
weights are manually tuned, VM prices are assumed static, and
trained policies do not generalise across cluster topologies.
This paper addresses all three and additionally reduces scheduling
overhead through four novel extensions. (1)~\textit{Bayesian
Simplex Search} (BSS) replaces manual weight tuning via Latin
Hypercube Sampling, achieving \textbf{+35.5\%} mean return over
the best fixed preset. (2)~Stochastic VM pricing via an
Ornstein--Uhlenbeck (OU) process enables price-aware scheduling,
reducing cost by \textbf{57.2\%} vs.\ FIFO. (3)~\textit{Job
batching} reduces scheduling overhead by up to \textbf{74\%} at
$B{=}8$ while achieving monotonic throughput improvement from
$B{=}2$ onward, reaching 0.32~tasks/s at $B{=}8$ (compared to
0.21~tasks/s at $B{=}1$).
(4)~\textit{Transfer learning} achieves \textbf{42.1\%} lower VM
cost vs.\ from-scratch DRL training and \textbf{1.5$\times$}
faster cost convergence on a 44\% larger target cluster, though
at the cost of a ${\sim}$43\% reduction in episode return due to
architectural mismatch from zero-padding the expanded state/action
space. Three ablation studies confirm each
improvement is causally attributable to the proposed mechanism.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Apache Spark, Distributional Reinforcement Learning, Rainbow DQN,

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Apache Spark \cite{spark} has become the dominant framework for
large-scale in-memory data processing, with organisations
increasingly deploying Spark clusters on cloud infrastructure.
Cloud-based scheduling is a multi-dimensional problem: minimise VM
cost~($\alpha$), maximise energy efficiency~($\varepsilon$),
resource utilisation~($\tau$), throughput~($\delta$), and deadline
adherence~($\mu$). Traditional heuristics such as FIFO and Round
Robin optimise only a single dimension.

Distributional DRL offers a principled framework here: rather than
estimating the expected return, distributional agents model the
full return distribution, yielding more stable training and better
uncertainty awareness~\cite{c51}. Verma et al.\ \cite{verma2025}
implemented C51 and Rainbow DQN~\cite{rainbow} for Spark
scheduling, demonstrating up to 63\% cost reduction over classical
baselines, but identified three limitations as future work:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Manual reward weight tuning}: fixed Dirichlet
    weights requiring domain expertise.
  \item \textbf{Static VM pricing}: real spot instances exhibit
    significant price volatility.
  \item \textbf{No cross-cluster generalisation}: full retraining
    required for differently-sized clusters.
\end{itemize}

This paper makes four contributions that directly address these:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Bayesian Simplex Search (BSS)}\footnote{Despite
    the name, BSS uses Latin Hypercube Sampling rather than a
    Gaussian-process surrogate; the term \textit{Bayesian} here
    refers to the budget-efficient, prior-informed sampling
    philosophy rather than strict Bayesian optimisation.}:
    gradient-free weight optimisation via LHS achieving +35.5\%
    return.
  \item \textbf{OU Spot Pricing}: non-stationary cost model
    enabling temporal arbitrage, $-$57.2\% cost vs.\ FIFO.
  \item \textbf{Job Batching}: $-$74\% overhead at $B{=}8$
    (+52\% throughput at $B{=}8$); recommended production setting
    $B{=}4$ achieves $-$63\% overhead with +24\% throughput and
    zero latency penalty.
  \item \textbf{Transfer Learning}: $-$42.1\% cost and
    1.5$\times$ faster cost convergence on a 44\% larger cluster,
    with a return trade-off (${\sim}$43\% lower than scratch) due
    to architectural mismatch from zero-padding the expanded
    state/action space.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

Islam et al.\ \cite{islam2020} applied DRL to Spark executor
placement for cost and time optimisation. Chen et al.\
\cite{chen2021} used actor-critic DRL for cloud resource
allocation; Kang et al.~\cite{transfer_cloud} applied adaptive
DRL to energy-efficient cloud task scheduling. These works
optimise one or two objectives; Verma et
al.\ \cite{verma2025} extended the paradigm to five simultaneous
objectives. Scalarising multiple objectives into a single reward via a
weighted sum is standard in multi-objective RL~\cite{moo_rl}.
Van Moffaert and Now\'{e}~\cite{moo_rl} demonstrated that sets
of Pareto-dominating policies outperform single scalarised
policies, motivating our search for an optimal weight vector
rather than relying on fixed presets.
Multi-objective weight tuning via evolutionary methods
is studied in~\cite{emo} but not applied to DRL reward shaping
via budget-efficient simplex search. OU-process cloud pricing was
modelled in~\cite{ou_cloud} and RL price-aware policies appear
in~\cite{rl_energy}, but neither has been applied to Spark
scheduling. Transfer RL~\cite{transfer_rl} and batch
scheduling~\cite{batching} are established in adjacent domains
but not in this combined distributional RL setting.

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Environment}

Following~\cite{verma2025}, the scheduling problem is a POMDP.
State $P = P_1 \oplus P_2$ combines VM resources and job
specification:
\begin{align}
  P_1 &= [vm^1_{\text{cpu}},vm^1_{\text{mem}},\ldots,
          vm^V_{\text{cpu}},vm^V_{\text{mem}}] \\
  P_2 &= [\text{jobID},\,e_{\text{cpu}},\,e_{\text{mem}},\,j_X]
\end{align}
Action space: $V{+}1$ actions (wait or place on VM $i$). The
five-objective reward is a weighted scalarisation on the
5-simplex:
\begin{equation}
  f = w_\alpha\alpha + w_\varepsilon\varepsilon +
      w_\tau\tau + w_\delta\delta + w_\mu\mu,\quad
  \textstyle\sum_i w_i=1,\;w_i\geq0
  \label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
scaled by $R_{\text{fixed}}{=}10{,}000$.

\subsection{Agents}

\textbf{C51}~\cite{c51}: categorical DRL over $N{=}51$ support
atoms in $[V_{\min},V_{\max}]$, minimising cross-entropy loss
against Bellman-projected targets.

\textbf{Rainbow DQN}~\cite{rainbow}: combines double Q-learning,
PER ($\alpha{=}0.6$, $\beta{=}0.4$), dueling networks,
multi-step learning ($n{=}2$), distributional RL, and NoisyNets.
Architecture: Conv($8{\times}8$/32, $4{\times}4$/64,
$3{\times}3$/64) + FC(200$\to$100$\to$51$\to$512).
The 51-neuron layer is not a hidden bottleneck but rather
the distributional output head, corresponding to the $N{=}51$
categorical atoms of the C51 distributional backbone; the
subsequent 512-neuron layer serves as the final policy head.
Training: 10,000 iterations (5,000 for Ext.~3--4), batch~128,
$\gamma{=}0.99$, replay buffer~10,000; NVIDIA RTX 2000 Ada.

\section{Extension 1: Bayesian Simplex Search}\label{sec:ext1}

\subsection{Motivation: Gradient Ascent Failure}

An initial attempt at online projected gradient ascent
(lr$_w{=}0.01$, update interval $K{=}100$ episodes) produced
completely flat weight trajectories. Within a 10,000-episode
budget fewer than 100 updates are possible --- insufficient for
5-simplex traversal --- and the noisy episodic reward provides
unreliable gradient estimates.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/extension1_weight_evolution.pdf}
  \caption{Dirichlet weight trajectories under projected gradient
    ascent over 10,000 episodes. All five weights remain flat,
    confirming gradient ascent fails to converge within the
    available training budget.}
  \label{fig:weight_evo}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Bayesian Simplex Search}

We replace gradient ascent with gradient-free LHS evaluation.
Note: despite the name, BSS uses Latin Hypercube Sampling rather
than a Gaussian-process surrogate; the term \emph{Bayesian} here
refers to the budget-efficient, prior-informed sampling philosophy
rather than strict Bayesian optimisation.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Bayesian Simplex Search (BSS)}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE Sample $N{=}20$ vectors via LHS on $[0,1]^5$;
       normalise each to sum to~1
\FOR{each candidate $w_i$}
  \STATE Train Rainbow DQN for 500 episodes
  \STATE Record $\bar{R}(w_i) =$ mean return (last 200 ep.)
\ENDFOR
\STATE $w^* \leftarrow \arg\max_i \bar{R}(w_i)$
\STATE Evaluate $w^*$ for 2,000 episodes vs.\ fixed presets
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

LHS guarantees uniform simplex coverage with $N$ evaluations.
Total budget: $20{\times}500{=}10{,}000$ episodes --- identical
to the gradient ascent baseline, making the comparison fair.

\begin{figure}[H]

The winning weight vector is:
$w^* = (0.178_\alpha, 0.101_\varepsilon, \mathbf{0.484}_\tau,
0.151_\delta, 0.086_\mu)$.
The dominant resource-utilisation weight ($\tau{=}0.484$) is
interpretable: maximising resource utilisation ensures efficient
VM packing, which cascades into higher throughput~($\delta$) and
return. The small deadline-adherence weight ($\mu{=}0.086$)
reflects the limited impact of adherence under the current
training budget (see Section~\ref{sec:discussion}). Direct cost
focus (fixed\_cost, $w_\alpha{=}0.50$) restricts exploration and
paradoxically achieves the lowest return (12.21).

\section{Extension 2: Stochastic VM Spot Pricing}\label{sec:ext2}

\subsection{OU Pricing Model}

VM prices follow an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process:
\begin{equation}
  dP_t = \theta(\mu-P_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t,\quad
  \theta{=}0.15,\;\sigma{=}0.20
  \label{eq:ou}
\end{equation}
with prices clamped to $[0.1\mu,3\mu]$. Fig.~\ref{fig:spot_prices}
shows the price time series for three VM types. Prices exhibit
mean-reverting volatility with occasional spikes to
${\approx}2.8{\times}$ base price, providing temporal arbitrage
opportunities for a price-aware agent.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/extension2_spot_prices.pdf}
  \caption{OU spot price time series for three VM types over the
    full training horizon. Prices are mean-reverting with
    volatility $\sigma{=}0.20$, reaching up to $2.8{\times}$
    base price and enabling temporal arbitrage.}
  \label{fig:spot_prices}
\end{figure}

\section{Extension 3: Job Batching}\label{sec:ext3}

\subsection{BatchScheduler Design}

A \texttt{BatchScheduler} wrapper accumulates up to $B$ pending
executor requests before invoking the agent, issuing $B$ VM
assignments per step. Rainbow DQN is trained for 5,000 iterations
at $B \in \{1,2,4,8\}$ ($B{=}1$ is the Phase~1 baseline).

\subsection{Results}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering\small
\caption{Extension 3 --- Overhead and Throughput vs.\ Batch Size}
\label{tab:ext3}
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}
\toprule
$B$ & \textbf{Overhead} & \textbf{Reduction} &
\textbf{Marginal Gain} & \textbf{Throughput} \\
\midrule
1 & 1.00 & ---  & ---  & 0.21 tasks/s \\
2 & 0.56 & 44\% & 44pp & 0.17 tasks/s \\
4 & 0.37 & 63\% & 19pp & 0.26 tasks/s \\
8 & \textbf{0.26} & \textbf{74\%} & 11pp & \textbf{0.32 tasks/s} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Fig.~\ref{fig:overhead_bar} confirms monotonic overhead reduction
consistent with $O(1/B)$ scaling. Diminishing returns are clear:
the $B{=}1{\to}2$ gain (44pp) is four times the $B{=}4{\to}8$
gain (11pp).

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/extension3_overhead_bar.pdf}
  \caption{Scheduling overhead ratio (agent calls / total executor
    placements) vs.\ batch size $B$ with $\pm$1 std.\ dev.\ error
    bars. Overhead reduces monotonically, consistent with $O(1/B)$
    theoretical scaling.}
  \label{fig:overhead_bar}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:throughput} shows job throughput vs.\ $B$ with
95\% CI ribbon. Throughput dips at $B{=}2$ (0.17~tasks/s, below
$B{=}1$'s 0.21~tasks/s) as the agent adapts to compound
decisions, before recovering monotonically: $B{=}4$ achieves
0.26~tasks/s and $B{=}8$ achieves 0.32~tasks/s --- a 52\% net
improvement over the $B{=}1$ baseline.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/extension3_throughput_vs_batch.pdf}
  \caption{Job throughput (tasks/s) vs.\ batch size with 95\% CI
    ribbon. The $B{=}2$ dip reflects adaptation cost; throughput
    rises monotonically from $B{=}2$ onward, reaching
    0.32~tasks/s at $B{=}8$ --- a 52\% improvement over $B{=}1$.}
  \label{fig:throughput}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:exec_cdf} shows that all batch sizes converge to
an identical execution time (${\approx}1.0$s), confirming that
overhead reduction incurs zero latency penalty.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/extension3_exec_time_cdf.pdf}
  \caption{Execution time CDF for $B \in \{1,2,4,8\}$. All

    time, confirming that batching reduces scheduling overhead
    without inflating job latency.}
  \label{fig:exec_cdf}
\end{figure}

We recommend \textbf{$B{=}4$} as the production optimum: 63\%
overhead reduction, 24\% throughput gain, and zero latency penalty.

\section{Extension 4: Transfer Learning}\label{sec:ext4}

\subsection{Target Cluster: ClusterEnv\_v2}

ClusterEnv\_v2 simulates a 44\% larger deployment: 13~VMs
(4~small, 4~medium, 5~large) vs.\ 9 in Phase~1; job demands
scaled $1.25\times$; state dimension 30 vs.\ 22; 14 actions.

\subsection{Training Conditions (5,000 iterations each)}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Scratch}: random init, lr$=9{\times}10^{-4}$.
  \item \textbf{Transfer-frozen}: Phase~1 weights loaded; Conv
    layers frozen; only FC layers trained.
  \item \textbf{Transfer-finetune}: Phase~1 weights loaded;
    all layers trained, lr$=9{\times}10^{-5}$.
\end{itemize}

Convergence is defined as the first iteration where smoothed cost
(window=50) falls below scratch's final cost median (50.81).

\subsection{Results}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering\small
\caption{Extension 4 --- Transfer Learning on ClusterEnv\_v2}
\label{tab:ext4}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Condition}&\textbf{Return}&\textbf{Cost}&
\textbf{$\Delta$ Cost}&\textbf{Conv.}\\
\midrule
FIFO-v2           & ---   & 15.00 & ---       & --- \\
Scratch           & \textbf{22.79} & 55.71 & ---  & 1,839 \\
Transfer-finetune & 13.42 & 35.14 & $-$36.9\% & 1,713 \\
Transfer-frozen   & 12.36 & \textbf{32.23} & \textbf{$-$42.1\%} &
\textbf{1,196} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

FIFO-v2's lower mean cost reflects incomplete job execution:
FIFO places fewer executors per episode (Return~=~---),
accruing less VM usage time and therefore lower raw cost.
The Return column confirms FIFO completes no schedulable
jobs (---), making its cost metric non-comparable to DRL
agents that achieve positive returns.
(Note: all transfer comparisons are relative to scratch DRL
training, not FIFO-v2, whose cost reflects incomplete job
execution rather than scheduling efficiency --- see
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.)

Fig.~\ref{fig:convergence_curves} shows dual learning curves. In
the cost panel (bottom), transfer-frozen (green dashed) crosses the
threshold at iteration~1,196 --- 643 iterations before scratch ---

Fig.~\ref{fig:cost_comparison} shows the cost box-plot for the
last 20\% of training. Transfer-frozen (green) has the lowest

(${\approx}12$--$13$) due to the architectural mismatch:
zero-padding the weight matrices for the expanded state/action
space ($22{\to}30$-dim obs, $10{\to}14$ actions) constrains the
transferred network's exploration. This is a known phenomenon in
transfer RL --- negative transfer in the primary metric can
co-occur with positive transfer in secondary metrics
\cite{transfer_rl}.

\subsection{Deadline Adherence = 0\%}

Strict deadline adherence requires complete job execution
(all executors placed and $\text{finish\_time} \leq
\text{deadline}$). Under the current 5--10k iteration budget,
agents achieve partial placements (${\approx}15$--$23$ valid
placements/episode) but rarely complete full jobs within deadline
windows. This is a training-budget limitation --- FIFO exhibits
identical characteristics --- and is expected to resolve with
extended training at 50,000+ iterations.

It should be noted that including $w_\mu = 0.086$ in the reward
function still shapes the placement policy even when $\mu = 0\%$
at the episode level, as partial deadline-proximity signals
influence step-level rewards. However, $w_\mu$ effectively
contributes no measurable episodic return under the current
budget, making the five-objective formulation functionally
a four-objective problem in these experiments.

\subsection{FIFO-v2 Cost Anomaly}

FIFO-v2's low mean cost in Table~\ref{tab:ext4} (15.00 vs.\
55.71 for scratch DRL) does not indicate superior scheduling.
FIFO places fewer executors per episode, accruing less VM usage
time and therefore lower raw cost, but completes no schedulable
jobs (Return~=~---). All transfer-learning cost improvements
reported in this paper are measured against scratch DRL training,
not FIFO-v2.

\subsection{Transfer Return Gap}

The return gap (scratch 22.79 vs.\ transfer ${\approx}12$--$13$)
arises from zero-padding weight matrices to bridge the 22-dim to
30-dim state mismatch. Progressive transfer (9$\to$11$\to$13
VMs) or adapter layers \cite{adapters} would reduce this in
future work.

The counterintuitive result that transfer-frozen outperforms
transfer-finetune on cost reduction ($-$42.1\% vs.\ $-$36.9\%)
despite finetune having more trainable parameters can be attributed
to regularisation via frozen features. When convolutional layers
are frozen, the network is forced to adapt only the FC layers to
the expanded state/action space ($22{\to}30$-dim obs,
$10{\to}14$ actions), constraining the hypothesis space and
reducing the risk of catastrophic forgetting. Transfer-finetune,
by contrast, allows all layers to shift, introducing instability
during the weight-matrix zero-padding adjustment phase and
effectively restarting feature learning in the early layers. This
is consistent with findings in NLP transfer
learning~\cite{adapters}, where frozen encoder layers often
outperform full fine-tuning on low-data regimes.

\subsection{Limitations}

All reported results are from single-seed runs, which is the
primary limitation of this work and the main barrier to
conference-level acceptance. Error bars and shaded confidence
intervals in all figures reflect within-run variance only, not
across-seed variance. Multi-seed evaluation ($N{=}5$ seeds) with
Welch's $t$-tests and Cohen's $d$ effect sizes would be required
to establish statistical significance for each reported
improvement. We identify this as the highest-priority direction
for future work. Single-seed GPU training at
10,000--5,000 iterations limits policy convergence; multi-seed
evaluation at 50,000+ iterations is recommended as future work.

%=====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper extended the distributional DRL Spark scheduling
framework of Verma et al.\ \cite{verma2025} across four
dimensions. Bayesian Simplex Search achieves +35.5\% return by
globally searching the weight simplex where gradient ascent fails.
OU spot pricing enables 57.2\% cost reduction over FIFO via
learned temporal arbitrage. Job batching at $B{=}4$ cuts overhead
by 63\% and improves throughput by 24\% with no latency penalty.
Transfer-frozen achieves 42.1\% lower VM cost vs.\ from-scratch
DRL training and 1.5$\times$ faster cost convergence on a 44\%
larger cluster, with a return trade-off (${\sim}$43\% lower than
scratch) attributable to zero-padding the expanded state space;
progressive transfer or adapter layers~\cite{adapters} are
identified as priority future work to recover this gap. Three
ablation studies confirm each improvement is causally attributable
to the proposed mechanism.

Future work: multi-seed statistical validation; progressive
transfer learning; cross-extension composition (BSS + spot pricing
+ batching + transfer); GPU training at 50,000+ iterations for
non-zero deadline adherence.
